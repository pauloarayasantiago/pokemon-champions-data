# How to Trick Room in Pokemon Champions

**Channel:** TrickRubyVGC | **Date:** unknown | **Views:** 1,403 | **Duration:** 14:04

---

Hello, my lovelies. Ruby here. And with the release of Champions, it's time for me to finally return to yapping on YouTube about my favorite archetype in Pokémon VGC, Trick Room. Pokémon Champions brings us the biggest VGC shakeup we've seen in years, as it's almost like the release of a new generation, but without new Pokémon or moves. Like many other players, I was originally flummoxed by the significantly reduced pool of Pokémon and items for trainers to work with. But over time in my initial session of the game, I grew fond of the lower power of the held item pool. I will say though, that the current selection of items and friends makes playing traditional hard Trick Room a lot more of a puzzle than it usually would be. I've had many friends ask me if I thought if it was even possible, to which I replied, "Of course it is." I played around with all sorts of different Trick Room ideas for Regulation MA, Regulation Mama Mia, what a small pool of usable things. And I found all sorts of things that I like. I'll talk about different setters and sweepers in this video without going into too much detail about teams, in addition to general tips and tricks for Trick Room in this format. Navigating Trick Room in this format can be a lot more difficult without the comfortable familiarity of Indeedee Hatterene and going follow me Trick Room helping hand expanding force. But that doesn't mean we cannot still ruin our opponent's day with Twisted Dimensions. So, here are some tips and tricks to keep in mind. Thankfully for Trick Room players, our sleep paralysis mushroom Amoonguss is not in the format. However, there's still two annoying Pokémon going around that spread sleep to be aware of, both funnily enough starting with a V. Both tend to be running Focus Sash, and there's no Goggles in the format. So, it may not seem at first like there's no obvious consistent answer. Well, thankfully there kind of is. All of the Trick Room teams, and Reg MA teams in general, that I have built at this time have had at least one Fake Out user. Because there's no Terastallization in this format, the sleep inducing Pokémon in front of you cannot Tera Ghost to escape being flinched. This is always safe to do because there's no partner that is paired with Venusaur or Vivillon that can one hit KO an Oranguru or Farigiraf. Like all formats, especially in best of one ranked ladder, you're always going to have players who are over prepared for Trick Room. So, you have to play around these guys. Thankfully, since Trick Room is not as crazy strong or popular in this format, Imprison is not super common. In fact, in all of my time laddering, I've only seen it twice. And once was on a Pokémon that doesn't normally run it, and really took me by surprise. Regardless of its scarcity, the method for playing around in prison is still effectively the same. Over time playing hard Trick Room, you'll gain a good sense for when an opponent is going to Imprison. When your spider off senses are tingling, don't waste your turn going for the wasted Trick Room, shove damage into the Imprisoner or take out the partner if possible. The Pokémon that sets Imprison has to summon on the field usually being useless in order to keep stopping your Trick Room. You can often take advantage of this by effectively treating the game as a 2v1. Your Trick Room sweepers are usually bulky enough to take a couple relevant hits and steal KOs back, since they don't have any stat points allocated to that pesky useless speed stat. Like Imprison, catching when an opponent will turn on or off Trick Room is a sixth sense that you'll pick up with time. But luckily, getting this one wrong isn't as bad, because if they turn off your Trick Room as you set it, they're still wasting a turn with that slot. When you see an opponent leading Sinistcha or Farigiraf, consider throwing extra damage into them before trying to set Trick Room, or even ignoring them and doubling into the partner. If you're running a Crabominable and see Sinistcha on the opposing team, consider leading it and throwing an Ice Hammer their way as well. Like most formats, Regulation MA feels like it has its anti-Trick Room mons. There's always at least one Pokémon that fits on many teams that happens to be also very strong into Trick Room. In Scarlet and Violet, some examples were Amoonguss, Iron Hands, and Rillaboom. In this early Champions format, it's feeling like Kingambit takes that role. A very bulky, very strong physical attacker that can and usually is slower than most good Trick Room threats. This sharp-witted chess piece can live many super effective hits, including fighting moves if packing Shuca Berry, and threatens to OKO many Trick Room sweepers in return. Kingambit is not an end-all nightmare answer for Trick Room, but every Trick Room team needs a consistent plan into it, even if it means simply running your own with Low Kick. Setting Trick Room in Regulation MA of Pokémon Champions is not always as free and easy as it could be in past formats of even much higher power level. No Spore or no Indeedee, Covert Cloak or Safety Goggles means certain usual answers to Trick Room can be quite a nuisance. So, I want to talk about which Trick Room setters I think are the best in the current format for ensuring our dimensions get twisted. Oranguru may not block priority moves like Farigiraf, but is still immune to Fake Out effectively with its Inner Focus ability blocking flinches. Having higher defenses but lower HP, Oranguru has a similar bulk profile to the Giraffe, but in my anecdotal experience, our little monkey friend has felt like it survives a wider array of hits than Farigiraf. What really makes Oranguru stand out though is Instruct. If you've ever played Paper Mario: The Thousand-Year Door, you may know that Goombella's Tattle Wink is one of the most broken partner attacks, trading Goombella's turn action to allow Mario an extra hit. Instruct does the same thing, and lets Oranguru's partner just smash through its opponents. For example, with Mega Crabominable's Ice Hammer, you effectively get a Calyrex Ice at home, being able to take the two KOs in one turn with a powerful Ice attack. Farigiraf has fallen a bit from grace as one of the most menacing Trick Room setters, but still holds its own near the top with its insane ability, Armor Tail. Protecting both Farigiraf and his partner from Fake Out, Armor Tail lets you get some extra damage or setup happening with the partner while our long boy twists the dimensions. As such, Farigiraf pairs well with Pokémon that are setting up on the first turn, such as Kingambit with Swords Dance or Hatterene setting Psychic Terrain, or next to a Pokémon that wants to slowly and safely pivot out, such as Incineroar. Without Throat Spray in this format however, Farigiraf does struggle a bit to set itself apart for dealing damage on its own, and functions best as a support next to a Pokémon that can get a lot done on its own. Mega Coalossal is also an excellent pairing for the Giraffe by getting powerful chip damage in even when the opponent protects. Sinistcha's usage on Trick Room teams has always been a bit up and down, but it's proving itself to be a reliable setter in both hard Trick Room teams and teams that have less dedicated Trick Room options, such as Archaludon Rain. This format has a lack of reliable redirection, and Sinistcha is one of the only redirectors that's not scared of either Sneasel or Sneasler options, being neutral to Dire Claw and immune to Close Combat. Sinistcha, of course, also supports many different team styles with its slew of tools, such as Life Dew, Strength Sap, Imprison, and its ability, Hospitality. What makes Sinistcha so uniquely useful in setting Trick Room is its typing. It's naturally immune to Fake Out due to being a Ghost, and while there is no Spore user in the format currently, Sleep Powder from Vivillon or Venusaur can be annoying, so Sinistcha's Grass typing still comes in handy there. The honorable mention for a Trick Room setter goes to Mimikyu. Mimikyu was a Pokémon I was initially excited to use on Trick Room in this format. Like everything else mentioned on this list, it cannot be bothered by Fake Out. Plus, it has Disguise to make killing it on turn one very, very difficult. The thing is, that's kind of where Mimikyu ends in terms of being useful at this time. Its pool of moves don't do a lot to support the sweepers we have currently in the format, and there's better ways to deal Fairy or Ghost damage on Trick Room teams. Mimikyu is still perfectly serviceable in this format, and I encourage you all to consider it when building still. It's just not quite as immediately obviously strong as the other three Pokémon in this list. In my search for the right special attacker to spam spread moves, I found Sylveon to be absolutely wonderful. Unlike Hatterene or Armarouge who want to be using Expanding Force, Sylveon does not require a partner to set up Psychic Terrain before letting off its brutal attacks. Sylveon is also more reliable than Torkoal at this job due to its Fairy typing. There are more Fire resists in the current format than Fairy resists, and a lot of the Fire resists also compare to Pokémon that change the weather, further reducing Torkoal's usefulness. Sylveon is also quite bulky. It lives stupid special hits that you would never expect, allowing it to steal early damage before Trick Room is up or finish the game when it's down. While striving with it, I even survived a Mega Glalie's Sludge Bomb at one point. With Pixilate giving a sizable boost to the damage of Normal-type moves it converts, Sylveon also gets a fantastic panic option in Hyper Beam. I wouldn't be running Hyper Beam if Sylveon had Choice Specs and was locking in its move, but it's not often all that difficult to survive the recharge turn and switch back to Hyper Voice. And so far, I've seen almost nothing survive a neutral hit from Hyper Beam. >> [music] >> Mega Crabominable has one of the most optimized stat spreads for Trick Room of any Mega Evolution, gaining points in only useful stats and even removing some pesky speed from the already low stat. Mega Crabominable is the perfect Trick Room machine. Posting an absolutely obscene 157 attack, Crab does a wonderful impression of Calyrex Ice in this low power format. Ice Hammer in tandem with Instruct or Helping Hand will KO almost anything, including resisted hits from Pokémon that are not particularly bulky, such as Basculegion, Mega Delphox, or Hisuian Typhlosion. For non-resisted hits, Ice Hammer OKOs all the Pokémon you associate with being typically frail, such as Mega Gengar or Mega Floette, and even quite a few things that may surprise you such as Mega Gardevoir or Sneasler. One of the craziest O-Cos Crabominable can get, albeit inconsistently, is with Mach Punch and the Kingambit. Against the right team, this can lead to an incredible blowout on turn one. I will admit that at the time of writing and recording this video, I have not personally used Mega Gholdengo in a large capacity, only playing around with it briefly to get footage. But this thing is an absolute monster. Sporting an even higher physical attack stat than Mega Crabominable and a sadly now nerfed Unseen Fist, this thing is one of the spookiest Ghost types Game Freak has given us in years. Gholdengo fills a lot of the same jobs as Mega Crabominable, but is not quite as slow as it and is weak to the crab itself, so it's worth keeping this in mind for Trick Room mirrors. Gholdengo also has slightly lower damage output because it lacks a boosting ability like Crab has in Iron Fist, but being able to shift through Protect is also fantastic. Overall, when picking between the two physical attacker Mega Evolutions for your team, it's going to come down to your needs for offensive and defensive coverage. Crabominable suits the team I'm currently running more, so it's the one I've been using the most. But Gholdengo won a tournament in the first week of Champions released and is absolutely no slouch either. Just how it's a counter to Trick Room because it's so slow, Kingambit is also a fantastic Trick Room sweeper for the same reason. It saw lots of usage in mixed Trick Room teams in Scarlet and Violet, and that's for good reason. Kingambit is in a fantastic slow speed tier and doesn't often rely on going first to dish out heavy damage since it's so bulky. With not so many ways to stave off physical damage besides Intimidate at the moment, Kingambit's Defiant ability makes it extremely difficult to disrupt. One of the most common ways to fight through Trick Room is to simply try and stall out the turns by using Protect, but Kingambit can punish this easily enough by stacking Swords Dance during the stall turns and ripping back with Sucker Punch once Trick Room is finished. While I did say Torkoal is underwhelming compared to Sylveon right now, our little tortoise is still a beast to be taken seriously when it shows up. Drought plus Eruption is a massively formidable offensive threat, and pairing it with Instruct on Oranguru can make Torkoal nearly impossible to wall. Torkoal still doesn't like all the opposing weather running around, so it works best on a team that's more dedicated to sun than just being slapped on a random team with a Trick Room mode. If you want to make the best use of Torkoal, it'd be best to consider pairing it with Venusaur and/or Mega Charizard Y for a fast mode on the team. As for an honorable mention, I'd also like to talk about Mega Slowbro. Mega Slowbro is a mon I think has a lot of potential for Trick Room trainers to make use of. Having Oblivious in its base form makes it immune to taunt, and when it Mega Evolves, it can dish out incredibly strong Expanding Forces and Body Presses. But overall, it feels like Mega Slowbro requires a lot of support to make it work in the current format. What it does have going for it is a very deep and diverse move pool and incredible bulk. It is so hard to kill this thing in one shot, so it's safe to set Trick Room quite easily even without redirection. If in Regulation MB we keep a similar power level to what we have now, but also within DD legal, we may see a format where Mega Slowbro can be an absolute menace.
