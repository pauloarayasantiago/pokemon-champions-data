# The ULTIMATE Pokemon Champions Team Building Guide

**Channel:** PuppyPown | **Date:** unknown | **Views:** 256 | **Duration:** 5:33

---

If you've ever been interested in competitive Pokémon, now's a great time to jump in. With the release of Pokémon Champions, building a competitive team from scratch or just experimenting with Pokémon and sets you've never tried before is easier than it's ever been. This video is your one-stop guide for everything the game offers when it comes to team building. The first thing you're going to need to do is recruit some Pokémon. After the tutorial, you should already have a small handful, but you'll want to recruit more to find the Pokémon that actually fit your team. In the recruiting menu, you get a fresh selection of Pokémon to choose from every 22 hours. If you don't want to wait, you can speed that up using tickets earned from completing missions or VP that you'll accumulate from battling. Before your options are revealed, there's a short little cutscene that's worth paying attention to. If you see some sparkles, that could mean there's a shiny Pokémon or other rare Pokémon in your selection. From there, you'll be shown around 10 Pokémon to pick from. You can add one as a free trial at no cost or permanently recruit one using tickets or VP. The key thing to keep in mind, once you pick one, the rest are gone until they happen to cycle back around naturally. So, take a good look before you commit. If you have Pokémon in previous games, you can also bring them over through Pokémon Home. I won't be covering that process in this video, but it's a great option if there's something specific you really want to use. Now, the Pokémon you recruit go into your box, but storage space isn't unlimited. You start with 30 slots. You can expand that a couple of ways. You can donate to Nintendo to purchase more space or you can earn extra slots by climbing the ranked ladder. Reaching the Great Ball tier gives you five extra spots, Ultra Ball gives you another five, and hitting Master Ball gets you 10 more on top of that. Once you have the Pokémon you want, it's time to train them. Head into the training section, select a Pokémon from your box, and you'll be shown a detailed view of everything you can customize. On the left side of the screen are your stat points. These are normally known as EVs, and these add a bonus point on top of your Pokémon base stats. You have 66 total to distribute with a maximum of 32 points per stat. Typical spread will have two stats at max investment and the other one using whatever's left over. You'll see this pattern a lot. You'll also notice the red and blue arrows on certain stats. These indicate the effects of your Pokémon's nature, which is shown as stat alignment in this menu. Clicking into it lets you change nature, which boosts one stat by 10% and lowers another by the same amount. If you're running a fast physical attacker, for example, you might go with Jolly or Adamant to boost attack or speed while dropping special attack, a stat that you're not really using anyways. One more thing to notice in the stats point view, if a stat is boosted by your nature, some of the investment bars will appear highlighted. Putting points into those highlighted bars gives you two points per bar instead of one, which can be really helpful when you're dialing in and optimize your customized stat spread. Next are the moves. This is fairly straightforward. Clicking on a move lets you replace it with anything that's in that Pokémon's learnset. The list is sorted by type by default, so you can scroll through to find what you need. You can also use ZL and ZR to change the sort order or apply filters to narrow things down further. On the right side of the screen, you'll see all the relevant details for each move, its category, physical, special, or status, along with power, accuracy, PP, effects, range, and anything else that might matter to you. Finally, you can change the ability. Some Pokémon only have one option here, but others will have up to three. The right side of the screen will show you what each ability actually does. And one last thing worth knowing about in the training menu, at the top of any of these screens, you'll see a battle data tab. Pressing Y will pull up the usage statistics for whatever you're currently looking at, whether that's stat spread, moves, or ability. This is genuinely one of the most useful tools in the game. If you're not sure what to run on a Pokémon, start here. Once your Pokémon are trained, head to the box to start assembling your team. Free to play, you have access to three team slots. You can add and change Pokémon freely, so don't feel locked in. This is meant to be flexible. On the right side of the box screen, you'll see a full stat readout for whatever Pokémon you're hovering over. When you click into a specific Pokémon on your team, you can assign it a held item. Items are team-specific, which is a nice detail. If you have Sneasel on two different teams, they can hold a Focus Sash on one or a White Herb on the other. When you open the item screen, your inventory will be organized into categories that you can cycle through with L and R, stat boosting, power boosting, defensive, recovery, effect extending, berries, and Mega Stones. One important rule, you can only have one of each item on a single team, so keep track of where you're placing things like your Sitrus Berry or Choice Scarf. If you're missing an item you want, you can go to the shop from the home screen, where held items and Mega Stones are available to purchase. One heads-up here, as of right now, a lot of items that are staples in mainline games aren't in the shop yet, so you may find yourself reaching for things that you wouldn't normally consider, like the Quick Claw or Bright Powder. Work with what's available. Now, back in the box, you'll also notice a train Pokémon option directly on the screen. So, if you need to make a quick adjustment without going all the way back to the training menu, you can do it right here. And then, if you click into the check summary, you'll get a clean view of your Pokémon along with their full stats. Pressing X from that screen brings up additional options. You can change the Pokémon's battle name, assign a title if it has one, or add it to your favorites. One last tip for you, if you're not sure what Pokémon to use, you can click into the battle section from home and then into battle data. This will be able to show you which Pokémon are the most popular in each format, either singles or doubles. If you look here, Incineroar, Sneasel, Garchomp, and Kingdra are really dominating double battles right now, so maybe consider adding a couple of these to your team to round out the cores. Once your team is set, you're ready to go. Just head into a battle, select your team, and get to it. If you found this helpful, a like and subscribe go a long ways. Thanks for watching, and I'll see you in the next one.
